Imagine a children’s show with a cute, pink, toddleresque protagonist. Let’s say, Kirby of the Stars, the most adorable superpowered weekend morning cartoon there is.

A glowing red celestial body is headed for the village…for the planet… and it is massive. Its surface resembles vast fields of lava, with a few dark streaks, like rivers and lakes on a molten map. It moves closer, and closer. The penguin king had a fortuneteller predict days earlier that he would burn in a great fire. The asteroid was spotted only later. The society is told it will be annihilated in 48 hours.

The “star” moves closer and closer, growing larger and larger in the sky. It has begun casting a red-orange tint on everything. The tint is more noticeable once it’s gotten large enough to blot out the sun, and the rest of the episode remains under this gloomy cast.

With its approach, volcanos erupt under the unbalanced gravitational forces, and the population flees to higher ground to escape lava flows, to one place that is hosting refugees: the castle. The king recently erected a statue of himself (instead of a park), and as he sees the citizens confessing wrongdoings to oneanother and making their peace in the courtyard, he realizes this was an ignoble action. He and his subordinates make a late rush to destroy the statue and erect the new park, with slides and swings and a jungle-gym, as a final gesture of benevolence.

He fetches who else but the titular character, the only one who he treats as an enemy, to show him the park. Kirby is delighted to see the park, and in his childish way is oblivious to the apocalyptic skyscape. He rushes to the swings, to have what must end up being his final moments of bliss, though he does not know it.

King Dedede, having this good gesture carried out, resigns himself peacefully to the swing aside Kirby. He sits and stares out at the orange landscape, and begins humming a melancholy tune to himself, a short phrase or two that rhyme in Japanese. Kirby keeps swinging casually beside him.

Great winds swell up, toppling trees and taking the two former foes backwards from their seats into the haze of a doomed distance, and the playground blows away…

Once, there was a small girl, named Sarah. On her birthday, she got a doll. She loved it and played with it all day. In the evening her parents said to leave the doll on the table, to let Rebecca (that’s the doll’s name) get used to the house. At night, Sarah heard a voice singing “Rebecca on the first step, Rebecca on the second step, Rebecca on the thrid step”. That’s when Sarah screamed for her daddy and mommy. They came in her room where she explained everything. They watched and found the doll on the third step of the stairway. The father said that they must’ve left it there and Sarah must’ve imagined the voice.

The next night she heard the voice again. “Rebecca on the fourth step. Rebecca on the fifth step” Again she screamed her lungs out. The parents watched again and then the father knew something was happening and took the doll and cycled to the nearest forest and burried it as deep as he could dig. The same night, Sarah heard the voice again. “Rebecca on the sixth step”. Sarah thought she imagined it, because the doll was burried. “Rebecca in front of your door”. Sarah still thought she imagined it and kept her eyes closed. “Rebecca in your room”. Sarah was getting nervous now, having sweat on her forehead, struggling to keep her eyes closed and to keep herself from screaming. “Rebecca on your bed”. Sarah again struggled to keep her eyes closed. “Rebecca next to your pillow”. Sarah opened her eyes and automaticly opened her mouth after seeing the sight, but no sound came out of it, for she was already dead.
