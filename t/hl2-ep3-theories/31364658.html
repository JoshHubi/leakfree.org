The reasoning behind creating episodes, aside from speeding up game development and learning from their mistakes early, was to bring the Half-Life 2 storyline to an end that befit﻿ Half-Life 3 enough so that it would not be a disappointment. I speculate Valve plans for Half-Life to be a trilogy and, therefore, to hurry up the series’ storyline and progression so they could finish it with one, final, 12-15 game that will be Half-Life 3. There was still so much to be told in the story of Half-Life, so many enemies to be introduced, that it would require 4 games to complete. Valve, however, desires a trilogy, in my opinion, and the Episodes were created to get their storyline where they want it to be to make a great, final game; to get their 4th game in their desired trilogy crammed in between Half-Life 2 and 3 under the name of Half-Life 2 episodes.

An argument supporting my idea:

The﻿ episodes of Half-Life 2 are called Half-Life 2: Episode X as opposed to Half-Life 3: Episode X; there’s a plain fact here: it’s not Half-Life 3. In my opinion, they wish to end the Half-Life 2 storyline at a specific point, and a specific cliff-hanger large enough to make an epic final game, as the first Half-Life ended. Beginning Half-Life 3 where Half-Life 2: Episode 1 began wouldn’t do so well, in my opinion, because the players wouldn’t want to see that goddamned citadel or city 17 anymore–they would’ve hoped to end all that in Half-Life 2.

Concluding Episode 3 with the fate of the Borealis (whether it be destroyed or kept, which I’m positive will occur in Episode 3) is a great cliff-hanger that Valve can proceed with an infinite amount of ways, much of which would lead to satisfactory storylines.

That is why I believe Valve created the Half-Life 2 episodes and incidentally named it Half-Life 2: episode X as opposed to Half-Life 3: episode X.
