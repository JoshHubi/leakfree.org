Ajax August 11, 2020, 3:29am #32

I tried to do something similar, but for all Xenofauna species. The entry I made for headcrabs went like this:

Headcrabs - Headcrabs are a unique creature. They are omnivorous, but have seemed to develop very carnivorous habits upon teleporting to Earth. This may be that their primary food source on Xen url=‘https://images4.wikia.nocookie.net/half-life/en/images/thumb/5/56/Fungus_on_Xen.jpg/800px-Fungus_on_Xen.jpg’[/url] is roughtly the same size and color of most of the humans found upon entry in the Black Mesa Research Facility, and, indeed, all over the world. The openings on the top of the fungus, combined with the headcrab’s “beak,” suggest that the fungus has a hard skin, and that the beak is needed to break it open to take the neutrients out.

Upon “feeding” on a human, however, the effects are quite different url=‘https://images3.wikia.nocookie.net/half-life/en/images/thumb/a/a0/Zombie_fire_arms_raised.jpg/418px-Zombie_fire_arms_raised.jpg’[/url], leading us to believe that the Xen fungus may be poisonous, and the antibodies produced by the headcrab are fed into the fungus, as well as acting passively within the headcrab’s own body. These antibodies are most definately lethal to a human, causing a parisitic symbiosis of headcrab/human relations. The human body is sustained by the headcrab, and the “zombie,” as the combined form is called, must take in neutrients at the expense of all other activities.

This change is not present in just the human body, however. Fluids ingested from the human body by the headcrab causes a mutation within the headcrab itself as well, creating an addiction to feed on proteins only present in Earth animals. If a headcrab cannot feed on humans, eventually, we imagine, they may return to their original state, though not unharmed, mentally. Studies of Dr. Kleiner’s pet headcrab could be of use url=‘https://images1.wikia.nocookie.net/half-life/en/images/c/c4/Lamarr_and_Kleiner.jpg’[/url].

Since the Black Mesa incident, other variations of headcrab have evolved. Whether these mutations were present in the headcrab’s “native”* environment or not remains to be seen, though some have argued that it is not too far fetched that these variations could have begun on Earth. The “Fast” headcrab url=‘https://images3.wikia.nocookie.net/half-life/en/images/6/6c/Headcrab_Fast.jpg’[/url] and the “Poison” headcrab url=‘https://images2.wikia.nocookie.net/half-life/en/images/e/e5/Headcrab_poison.jpg’[/url].

The Headcrab life cycle is still a mystery, though we know that a creature called the “Gonarch” url=‘https://images4.wikia.nocookie.net/half-life/en/images/2/2a/Gonarch_model.jpg’[/url] is resposible for their birth, we have no idea as to whether or not they are born asexually or if the Gonarch has a mate. We also do not know how long it takes a headcrab to reach the Gonarch stage, though the current estimation is under 20 years, if the Fast and Poison headcrab variations were born on Earth. It is assumed, as well, that very few headcrabs make it to the Gonarch stage.

Headcrabs tend to stay near to their “mother” at the early stages of their life url=‘https://images1.wikia.nocookie.net/half-life/en/images/a/a3/Headcrab_baby.jpg’[/url]. They do not appear to leave their nest until after they’ve reached their main developmental stage. After they reach their main life stage, they leave the nest and find their own little caves to live out their lives. Headcrabs seem to live in mostly cavernous areas, often with very little room to move, but almost always with a food and water source nearby.

Further observations on this species are needed to determine timespans and mating habits.
