Anyway, I’ll let part of a review of the game I posted tell you why precisely the game is good:

Half-Life 2 is a master-class in minimalistic storytelling. There are no cutscenes that pull you away from the action- the entire game is experienced through Gordon’s eyes at all times. To many developers this may seem like an unnecessary constraint, as story development is limited purely to what the player can see and hear. But Valve worked within these limits with an incredible degree of intelligence, and the overall experience of Combine-controlled Earth is at once startlingly realistic and subtle in its desperate climate. The coastline outside city limits is positively chilling- the water has receded a great deal, as dockyards and old ships lie rusting on the shore. The canal water ranges in quality from muddy to radioactive, and seawater is infested with parasitic leeches. A man in the opening train station rants quietly to himself about the mysterious goings-on in the train system as the Civil Protection officers beat a man for trying to move his own luggage. The Combine-built walls gradually “eat” the old city by moving about and tearing down the antiquated Russian architecture. Nova Prospkt’s empty prison walls play host to Combine medical experiments and the indoctrination of new soldiers. All of these elements combine to create an atmosphere that feels familiar and yet threatening; Earthly but alien.

Despite the heady atmosphere, Half-Life 2 at its core is an evolution of classic first-person shooter gameplay. Freeman has two health meters- his actual “HEALTH” and the Hazardous Environment Suit (HEV)’s “SUIT” battery. The suit helps Gordon soak up damage from small arms and explosions, and as long as the player can find health packs, batteries, and/or chargers for both meters, the player is relatively safe in exploring the game’s linear level layouts. The difficulty curve is practically invisible, and the player is given assistance with the controls as they play- for instance, in the opening sequence when the player must escape a Combine interrogation room, the interface is utilized to instruct the player to use the “use” key to stack boxes. Gordon must survive by his wits and his acquisition of an arsenal of weapons given to him by allies and looted from the corpses of his enemies. The arsenal at first glance is rather limited, consisting of only a few types of guns and explosives, but what really sets Half-Life 2 apart is the Zero-Point Energy Field Manipulator, known to the player as the “Gravity Gun.” The Gravity Gun allows the player to pick up virtually any physically enabled object and either throw it or set it somewhere. The gun feels fantastic- the punting feature sends objects flying downrange with a satisfying kick to the view, and objects can be pulled over and grabbed from a fairly long range. Players can use the gun in a variety of offensive and defensive situations, as anything from an impromptu grenade launcher to a bullet shield. The weapon lends itself to a variety of play-styles which sets it apart from just about any weapon in any game before or since.

The game’s arsenal will certainly come in handy, as the player is up against a wide variety of opposition throughout the game. The city metropolice and Combine regular soldiers are the primary opposition, leveraging many of the game’s weapons at a variety of ranges. Snipers take positions in high windows, keeping a stern watch and a high powered rifle trained on nearby enemies. Police units carry portable flying sawblade robots called “Manhacks” that fly in swarms. The Combine’s military might also allows them to send a variety of ground and aerial vehicles into combat- dropships can rapidly mobilize a squad of eight soldiers into action at a moment’s notice, gunships can rain down death from above with their heavy cannons, helicopters can both spray bullets and drop mines on the player in the game’s “Water Hazard” chapter, and Combine APCs can fire long range missiles at opponents. Additionally, the Combine utilize alien fauna as biological weapons, firing canisters of headcrabs (the game’s equivalent to the Alien film’s “facehugger”), which take over human hosts and walk about as zombies. On the coast, massive flying bugs dubbed “Antlions” rule the sands, mercilessly perusing any player who trespasses on their territory.

Despite the relentless pace of the combat encounters, Half-Life 2 knows when to let the player branch out a bit. Hidden throughout the game are “Lambda Caches,” areas where resistance members have stashed supplies. With proper resource management and careful aim, players can expect a phenomenally well balanced 12 hour experience, adjustable to three difficulties. The difficulty setting sadly doesn’t change the AI to any substantial degree; it merely modifies player and enemy damage settings. By modern standards, the AI can also feel rather stupid at times- Combine soldiers may sometimes leave perfectly good cover to pressure the player by rushing, and a well known exploit locks the soldier in melee animations while the player can chip away at their health from point blank range, but overall the AI manages to do quite well at using team tactics against the player.

Overall, Half-Life 2 earned 30+ game of the year awards for a reason- the fantastic art design, smart programming, and intelligent writing combine for a uniquely enthralling experience. Simply put, this game is not to be missed. Anyone even remotely interested in game design should pick up Half-Life 2 and its Episodes, as Valve has updated the game with commentary nodes, allowing for a look “behind the scenes” in-game that is exhaustive in its detailed analysis of some aspects of the level design and the programming that went into them.
