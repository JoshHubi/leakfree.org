Ways to Maximize the Nutrient Retention of Your Foods

[COLOR=‘Red’]Eat local - The less time your food spends in storage, on the back of a truck or on the shelf, the more nutrients you’re going to conserve. Check your local farmers’ markets and ask where the food is coming from. Favour foods that have travelled under 100 miles.

[COLOR=‘Red’]Don’t Forget Raw - Eating foods raw is the best way to maximize how many nutrients are left intact. Salads are something that can be eaten daily as part of a meal, or as the meal itself. Smoothies are another way to get a good dose of raw foods. The blending of the food does expose it to oxygen, therefore destroying some of the nutrients, but the majority of the nutrient content is still intact.

[COLOR=‘Red’]Cook Lightly - The longer a food is exposed to heat, the more nutrient loss there is. Cook vegetables lightly, so that they’re still crisp and colourful. Steam rather than boil, if you can. Fish should only be cooked to medium to preserve essential fats. Garnish long-cooked stews or soups with raw and fermented veggies to add important enzymes and nutrients.

Ferment - Unlike other food preparations, lactofermentation actually increases the nutrient content of foods. The process of the bacteria breaking down the foods adds vitamins, enzymes and more probiotic bacteria. Fermented foods sold in the grocery store have been pasteurized, however, killing these enzymes and beneficial bacteria, but home fermented foods are extremely nutrient-dense.

[COLOR=‘Red’]Skip the Processed Convenience Foods - Canned, boxed or pre-cooked foods are going to have the least nutrients left intact. The more processing (i.e., the less it looks like the original food) the less nutrients are retained. Frozen or dried foods are better, but fresh is best. Some processed foods have vitamins added back in, but this is a far inferior approach to eating than getting the naturally occurring nutrients from the original food.

Some nutrients are remarkably resistant to cooking or processing. The anthocyanins, pigment phytonutrients found in fruits and veggies (like purple potatoes) seem to be affected very little by heat. Others, though, are quite fragile, like the essential fatty acids, which begin to oxidize instantly on exposure to air.

[COLOR=‘Red’]But the important thing is not to obsess over nutrient loss. Yes, some nutrients are lost in the cooking of your foods, but this isn’t the end of the world. As long as you’re eating fresh ingredients, lightly cooked or carefully prepared, you’re still getting a significant nutrient profile from your foods.
