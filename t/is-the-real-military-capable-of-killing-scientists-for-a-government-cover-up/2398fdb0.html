Okay…

While the world trade centre was thought to be one of the most structurally stable buildings. This was due to the design using the strength of the core to support the weight, and the walls to support lateral forces (wind loading). The two were connected by steel trusses. When steel is exposed to heat, it becomes very soft, as I’m sure you know. Concrete is traditionally used to clad steel to make it fireproof, but in the case of the world trade centre drywall was used to save on weight. Upon the impact of the plane, the drywall shattered, exposing the core to heat

All well and good so far, the building could have stood up fine with the impact damage. It was designed to withstand a hit from a Boeing 707, which was the largest airliner at the time of design, so the size of the plane wasn’t an issue.

What was an issue is that in the design the fuel payload of the aircraft wasn’t accounted for. When specifying materials for a building, fire resistance is one of the 7 considerations, and the specification includes anything flammable that is kept in the building (paper, carpets, whatever), but unfortunately didn’t include a few tons of jet fuel. The core was exposed to this heat, due to the shattered drywall, and the trusses between the walls and core began to sag and fail. As they came away, they collapsed onto those below, beginning what is known as a progressive collapse, where each floor collapses onto the one below. The ‘explosives’ that people see are evidence of the trusses being pulled away from the outside wall
