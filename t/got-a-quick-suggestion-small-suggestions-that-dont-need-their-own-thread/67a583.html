HiggsJetHarmSig2.mp3

Enjoy!

edit: Also available uncompressed: HiggsJetHarmSig2.wav.zip

edit: Licensed under Creative Commons.

edit: I just read up on how the sound is made. Basically, the reaction/decay, called a “jet”, tends to consist of a series of separate events, each one isolated. Each event is called a “cell”. Each cell has three values associated with it: How much energy was released in that cell, how much time had elapsed from the start of the decay before the cell formed (“distance”), and how far the cell diverged from the initial trajectory of the particle (“dR”). The following picture (from the LHC Sound web site) sums this up:

So, to create this sound, the so-called “harmonic signature”, they used certain criteria to compute a frequency from each cell, and then they played back a synthesized tone on each one of those frequencies, all at once. That’s what you’re listening to.

Of course, there are three different sounds in the audio file, each one about 20 seconds long. The three sounds correspond to three different ways of computing the frequency.

1: The dR value of each cell is interpreted as a frequency.

2: The “distance” of each cell (how much time has elapsed from the start of the reaction) determines the frequency, and then presumably the amplitude is modulated by the intensity.

3: The intensity of each cell determines its frequency. Thus in the third sound, what you hear is a map of the energy levels across all cells collected. This is useful because cells, I believe, correspond to particular types of subatomic particles decaying, and each type will always release a specific amount of energy. Thus, this set of tones tells you what the component parts of the initial particle were, or something.

The other thing to keep in mind is that this data, and thus the resulting sound, is based on simulations they’ve run, and not actual collected data. It’s in theory what a Higgs Boson decay sounds like.

That’s my understanding of it, anyway. The LHC Sound explanation seems to assume some prior knowledge of theoretical physics and particle mechanics.
