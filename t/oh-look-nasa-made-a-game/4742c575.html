I don’t know if the matter of seasons has ben cleared up, and I don’t really care.

Yes, technically the tilt of the earth makes one hemisphere closer to the sun. But this is negligable. It makes no difference in temperature whatsoever.

The culprit for varying temperatures is the angle in which the light strikes the earth. Let’s look at the poles for a bit. Cold, desolate. Yet in the summer the sun never sets. Why don’t the poles melt? The answer is the angle at which the sun strikes the ground. At the intense angle of the poles, most of the heat simply glances off the ground, never to be absorbed. In short, the greater the angle, the less light is absorbed, and the more light bounces off. This is why the equator is so warm. There is nearly a 90 degree angle between the ground and the sun. This is also why noon is the hottest part of the day: the sun is directly overhead at that time.

Now, as the earth tilts during seasons, the angles at which the sun strikes the ground are altered. Right now, the angle at which the sun hits the earth is closer to 90 degrees at the northern hemisphere than the southern. So the northern hemisphere experiences summer, whereas the southern experiences winter.
