In a general sense, yeah. In no particular order: we’re tying up loose ends, replacing outdated content, polishing unfinished features/areas/assets/mechanics, fixing bugs, optimising performance/load times/memory utilisation, preventing potential crashes, negating exploits, improving consistency in terms of art direction/gameplay/pacing, tweaking the difficulty, reworking things that aren’t fun/intuitive…

The last few percent of the development takes up a disproportionate amount of time because the aim is extremely diffuse. There are hundreds of tiny tasks to attend to, and only a small number of people can do them.Not any single one, but there are lots that allude to what’s going on at any given moment. Raminator:

Game design would be easy if you had full control over everything the player did and saw; but then you’d be making a movie and not a video game.Longer than that, but it’s a dynamic process. There’s a bit more involved to it than just reactionary fixes.Progress hasn’t been great, and it has slowed down a bit. That was never a secret. Nevertheless, work is being done, tasks are being completed, and progress is being made.

With regards to digeridude not having anything to do, it’s exactly like mankyman said. There’s very little prop modelling work to be done these days, and that’s been the case for a very long time (the amount of new content that you require obviously drops off as you acquire said content). Black Mesa’s developers have also always needed to be more proactive than you’d expect too, it’s never really been a case of simply having someone in charge telling you what to do and how to do it and when to do it by. We’re not a proper company, we don’t have compatible schedules, we can’t work like that.
